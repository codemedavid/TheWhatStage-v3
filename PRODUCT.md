# WhatStage

## Product Purpose

WhatStage is a lightweight CRM workspace for small sales teams and solo operators who run their pipeline through messaging channels (Facebook/Messenger today, more later). It unifies inbound conversations, lead status, custom fields, and stage progression in one quiet, fast surface — so a single operator can hold a hundred deals in their head without losing context.

It is not a full enterprise CRM. It is the tool a founder, agency owner, or two-person sales pod reaches for when HubSpot is too heavy and a spreadsheet is too lossy.

## Register

product

The leads page is a workspace surface — operators live in it daily. Design serves throughput, not decoration. Marketing/landing pages live elsewhere and use the brand register.

## Users

**Primary: the operator-owner.** Founder, agency lead, or sales-of-one running 20–150 active leads. Spends 1–3 hours/day in the pipeline. Cares about: speed of capture, where a deal sits, what to do next, exporting to reports. Power user of keyboards. Hates click-through depth.

**Secondary: the small sales team (2–6 people).** Shares one pipeline. Cares about: who owns what, stage hygiene, handoffs, weekly review.

Both are fluent with modern tools (Linear, Notion, Superhuman). They notice and reward craft. They abandon tools that feel slow, cluttered, or make them think about the tool instead of the work.

## Strategic Principles

1. **Throughput over discoverability.** Frequent actions (move stage, add lead, edit field, search) are one keystroke or one click. Settings and configuration are deeper but reachable.
2. **Density without noise.** Show many leads at once, but let the eye rest. Whitespace is not wasted — it's the pause between signals.
3. **Calm under load.** A pipeline with 200 leads and 8 stages should feel as composed as one with 12 leads. Visual weight scales sublinearly with data.
4. **One accent, many states.** Emerald is the only color that means "interactive." Status comes from typography, position, and tiny chromatic hints — never from filling cards with color.
5. **The drawer is a workspace, not a popup.** Editing a lead is the most common action. It deserves real estate, real keyboard handling, and real focus discipline.

## Brand Voice & Tone

Plainspoken, confident, undecorated. Short labels. No marketing language inside the product. "Add lead" not "Create new opportunity." "Move to Won" not "Mark as successfully closed." Empty states are direct and useful, not cute.

## Anti-references

- **HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive** — too many features visible at once, color used as decoration, every card screaming for attention. We are the opposite.
- **Generic SaaS dashboards with hero stat tiles** — Big-Number-Plus-Sparkline-Plus-Trend-Arrow is a cliché. The leads page is a workspace, not a brag wall.
- **Trello** — too playful, too colorful, columns become a craft project. We are quieter.
- **Notion databases** — too generic, too configurable, no opinion about what a sales pipeline should feel like.

Closer to: **Linear's project view, Superhuman's split, Height's tasks.** Quiet, dense, keyboard-driven, tasteful.

## What "good" looks like for the leads page

- An operator can see 30+ leads across stages without scrolling.
- Adding a lead takes <5 seconds and never leaves the page.
- Switching between board and table feels instant and preserves filters.
- The active drawer feels like a desk surface, not an interruption.
- The page never shouts. The only saturated color is emerald, used sparingly.
- Bulk actions, search, filters, and stage management all feel like first-class citizens, not bolt-ons.
